Anna Barker (link), a published author and academic writing tutor, developed ALEX while working with university students as a Royal Literary Fund (link?) Writing Fellow. Many of the problems students presented her with stemmed from not having a clear plan for their essay.

What do I need to get started?

  • 4-6 highlighter pens in different colours.
  • A pencil and an eraser.
  • A roll of brown paper to draw your plan on. A3 paper is also fine.
  • A magazine folder (an empty cereal packet with the top cut off diagonally works just as well)
  • A shelf or an area where you can stand up your books.

ABOUT THIS STEP
An overview to the structure map by Dr Anna Barker. Length: 1 minute

Make sure you have read the step called about this app. It will tell you what materials you need and give you a useful overview of how the app works. Now to your essay. A common mistake when students get their essay question or assessment criteria is to start writing straight away. Time spent familiarising yourself with the task set is well spent. By really understanding what’s been asked of you, both your reading and your writing becomes very focused. Let’s look at our example of assessment criteria. It’s quite dense, isn’t it? I wonder if we can’t make it a bit more friendly? Rewriting the individual tasks is a good way of making sure you understand them. Of course you need to be careful not to inadvertently change the task, but changing a few words for simpler ones that mean the same thing can really help to cement in your mind what it is you need to do. Here’s the same assessment criteria rewritten. Do you know what all the different essay terms mean? These are the verbs – the words that tell you what to do. Describe;  Define;  Compare;  Analyse. Make sure you understand their different meanings. When you’re done don’t bury the essay question or assessment criteria in a folder. Pin it up, put it on your wall, stick it to the front of your folder. Make it visible.

TASKS
  • Take ownership of the assessment criteria/essay task. Make sure you understand it. If it’s a question make sure you understand what it asks of you.
  • Use words you don’t understand? Ask your tutor. What does ‘describe’ mean? How is it different from ‘evaluate’, ‘review’, ‘compare’, ‘reflect’? These verbs are your ‘to do’ list for the essay so you need to make sure you’re going to be doing the right things. Check out the glossary to be sure.
  • If you need to, REWRITE the tasks into simple sentences you are more comfortable with. You could also try to rephrase a statement as a question.
  • Then pin it up on your wall so you can keep referring back to it and make sure you’re staying on track.
