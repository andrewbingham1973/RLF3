INTRODUCTIONS

Your introduction (often around 10 per cent of your word count) orientates your reader, tells them what to expect, and what themes you will be exploring. It also tells them the order in which to expect it. Think of it as a menu: first I’ll serve you garlic mushrooms, then a delicious salmon wellington, and finally, a slice of mandarin cheesecake! It might also set out your thesis, or argument and outline how you are going to approach it.

The introduction starts wide in context and then gets narrower. The funnel shape on your map is to remind you of this. So the first sentence (or sentences) set the scene for your essay, they position the content (your response to the particular essay task) into the big picture (your field of study). These sentences are often called context statements or context sentences. They should be inviting for your reader, establish the importance of the topic for the discipline whilst indicating which aspect of the field you are looking at. Good context sentences/opening sentences are engaging: they grab your reader and make them want to read on.

Context statements are wide in context and establish the importance of the topic for the world/society/discipline…

Context statements are wide in context and establish the importance of the topic for the world/society/discipline…

Examples:
a) The demands on the modern day social worker…
b) In the history of development economics, X… has been thought of as a key factor in….
c) X is at the heart of our understanding of….
d) X and Y have been an object of research since the 1960s…

As the funnel gets narrower so does the focus of your introduction. So the next sentence/s describe the scope and content of your essay. You can mention the themes of your essay in these sentences. Don’t worry if you don’t know what they are yet, we’ll think about that in the next two steps.

Examples:

a) This essay will focus on/examine/give an account of….
b) This essay seeks to remedy these problems by analysing the literature of….
c) This assignment examines the significance of X in the rise of….
d) This essay critically examines/discusses/traces….

So your introduction sets the scene but it’s also the menu for your essay. It says what you are going to do in the order you are going to do it. The main body of the essay will stick to that order.

SETTING OUT AN ARGUMENT

As you become more confident your introduction might tell the reader the slant of your essay – you may choose to argue a particular stance or weigh up the pros and cons of a theory or method.

You’ll tell the reader the thrust of your argument in your introduction and then spend the rest of the essay persuading them of that view by presenting them with a logical line of reasoning which explores all the evidence. This will be organised into points and represent a thorough analysis of the literature that both favours your argument and acknowledges alternative viewpoints.


Examples:
a) The purpose of this paper is to review recent research into the….
b) In this paper I argue that….
c) In the pages that follow, it will be argued that….
d) This paper attempts to show that….
e) In this essay, I attempt to defend the view that….

CONCLUSIONS

Just as your introduction is the menu that dishes up what your essay will serve, your conclusion helps them see why all your analysis and information matters. Conclusions propel your reader to a new view of the subject. Your conclusion doesn’t introduce any new ideas, rather it sums up everything you’ve written. Specifically, your conclusion should:

  • Revisit the main idea/s of your essay.
  • Summarize the main points of your essay.
  • Leave your reader with a positive final impression.

TIPS
  • You can write your introduction at the start of the writing stage and then redraft it later on, or leave it until the end. It’ll almost certainly change though as the structure of the rest of your essay takes shape.
  • It’s okay NOT to know what your context/opening sentence might be now. Don’t waste time pouring over what it should be, but remember that as you are reading and researching you can be thinking about what that wide, context statement/s could be – and make a note.
  • It’s also okay NOT to know what your particular stance is (what you think) now. This will develop as you read. It’s good practice (regardless of your essay task) to write down what you actually think about what you’re reading and to change your mind several times. No referencing required, just you, in your notebook, deciding what you think.
  • A conclusion is the opposite of the introduction - remember the introduction begins wide in context and ends specific. The conclusion begins specific (the points of your essay revisited) and ends wide (wider implications, further research needed etc). ALEX is going to help you organise your points into paragraphs so it will be easy to follow the main points of your essay and summarise them in the conclusion.
  • Conclusions must be written after you have written and redrafted your essay.
  • They must not introduce any new information, rather it is a summary of the main points or recommendations.
