ABOUT THIS STEP
xxxxxx by Dr Anna Barker. Length: xxxx minutes

Brainstorming is a prewriting method designed to explore what you know about a topic and what you don’t know. Like freewritingit’s a stream of consciousness technique in which you rapidly record your ideas. There’s no reading involved prior to you completing the brainstorm and all ideas are equal. The goal is simply to get as many of your thoughts about a topic onto a sheet of paper. It’s good for loosening up your mind and allowing your subconscious to work on an issue. It’s also an opportunity for you to record some of your own questions or beliefs about a subject before you fully explore the literature.

The brainstorm I’m asking you to do in ALEX is a little bit different. It has many of the benefits outlined above, but it’s also the beginnings of a STRUCTURE MAP for your essay. It asks you to think about your material differently. Right from the outset, you’re going to think in terms of ‘points’ you can make in your essay. You might be more used to thinking in terms of ‘content’ i.e I need to include information on x, y, z. Thinking in points is a small but hugely valuable shift in thinking.

Example: Let’s say your essay asks you to look at child poverty. Thinking in terms of content you might write down things like unemployment, school meals, social security. But thinking in points, you would write down “child poverty increases when both parents are unemployed?” or “Research suggests free school meals are essential nutrition for children affected by poverty?” Notice I’m adding a question mark at the end of my point because I don’t know if it’s accurate yet, I haven’t done the reading! But it’s a possible point, one that I can explore further when I begin reading. Points are what you will later flesh out into paragraphs, properly referenced with evidence and accompanied by analysis.

TASKS

Drawing your map

  • Take a sheet of paper for your brainstorm. Make it large, at least A3. I use brown paper because it’s cheap and big enough.
  • Remember your rewritten assessment criteria? There are usually three or four areas of study you will be assessed on, but there might be as many as six. Let’s write them up on your paper and give each one a colour. Remember to use colours that match the post-it notes you have. You’ll need to spend a few minutes thinking about the themes; use the assessment criteria to guide you. What are the different topic areas your essay lends itself to?
  • Look at our example of the Structure Map to guide as to how it should look. You might even like to give your introduction section a colour – just in case you come across a reference or a quote that could usefully be used as context statement (more on context statements in Step 4).

Complete your brainstorm

  • Using a pencil, you can now begin your brainstorm for each area of your assessment criteria/essay themes. Chances are you will already have some idea of what to include. What do you already know about the topics? You may even discover that you have existing views on particular topics. These are worth making a note of as they could form the basis of an argument, but be aware you might change your mind as you explore the literature and think more deeply. Don’t worry if you don’t write down much at this stage, it’ll grow as you read.
  • And remember to think of points you could make in your essay NOT simply content. Write them down on your plan under the appropriate theme.
  • Look at our example of the Structure Map to see how it will look with your points added.

TIPS
  • Thinking in terms of points puts you in a critical analysis frame of mind rather than an information delivery frame of mind. Delivering and describing evidence will get you marks, but presenting a point that you underpin with a critical evaluation of the evidence is likely to do better. So what points can you make a note of that meet the Assessment Criteria? What points can you make that you will seek evidence to underpin when you start reading?
  • Remember that what you put on your brainstorm at this stage is just what you know now. If it isn’t much, don’t worry; the remainder of your points will be identified during the reading stage.

Examples of points vs content


Content: Sylvia Plath’s role
Point: Sylvia Plath became a figurehead for feminists of the time?


Content: Brainstorming.
Point: Students who brainstorm essays get better marks?
