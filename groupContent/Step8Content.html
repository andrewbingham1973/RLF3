This step is about creating flow in your essay. Flow occurs at a structural level (how you arrange your themes and paragraphs) and at sentence level. Imagine you are creating a path for your reader that will haul them through your research/argument. You need to make that path as easy to follow as possible.

  • Complete this for each of the colours on your plan.

  • Linking phrases and signposting are techniques you can use to help achieve a smooth order. Think of them as the connective tissue between the different themes of your essay and the points you make. These ‘joins’ don’t just come from your assessment criteria or tasks you have been set though, they come from your own analysis of the literature. For example, those two studies that appear to contradict each other, or the sources that agree on a certain methodology and therefore offer a common link.

IDEA - structuring your paragraphs

Once you have decided the most logical order to present your points (remember, each of your points is a potential paragraph) you can think about how you might write them to showcase your research.

I.D.E.A is a suggestion for how you might structure your paragraphs. It stops you from ever writing a paragraph that contains no point, or one that has a point but insufficient evidence, or that has evidence but lacks analysis. It’s a handy way of presenting material that foregrounds your points and prevents you from missing out the essay material that gets you marks.

  • I – Identify your point. Use the first one or two sentences to clearly state the point you are making. A point that contributes to your overall argument/meeting of your assessment criteria.
  • D – Add to detail to your point, or a Definition might be necessary. This is the space to further clarify that point, if needed.
  • E – Evidence. What evidence do you have that underpins your point? Where in the literature does it support or contradict the point you are making? Describe the study/theory/approach.
  • A – Analysis. What is the significance of what you’ve described? How might it have been done differently? Are there flaws in this thinking? Refer to your critical prompts.

You should try to give greatest weight in your paragraph to your critical analysis. In time you might look to writing enough critical analysis of the point made to give it a paragraph entirely for itself.

Those reading your essay will benefit from a clear pattern – point, evidence, analysis. It will make it easier to follow and give the impression you have control over your material.
