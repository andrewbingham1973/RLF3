ABOUT THIS STEP
xxxxxx by Dr Anna Barker. Length: xxxx minutes

Your wide read should have given you a feel for what is out there in the literature for your topic areas. You may have noticed what the different arguments are (even if not in detail) and what evidence exists for them. Better still you may have identified some points you could make to show your understanding of this literature in relation to your assessment criteria. This step asks you to read this material again, but one theme at a time and more closely. Close reading is about applying a deeper level of critical thinking to the material. Be on the look out, too, for ways in which you might link these different areas of your essay together. Can you see a spine developing for your essay?

Each theme/colour on your Structure Map will contain a number of sources. You will carry out this close read of all the material for each colour one at a time. This second read is done more slowly. Don’t rush this step. Think critically and carefully about the material you read, discuss it with others, make notes and, if you need to, add to your reading material to flesh out any gaps in your thinking.

TASKS
  • Now begin your CLOSE read. Take everything (chapters, journals, notes) that is BLUE down from your shelf and spread it out around you. Read it again.
  • Think about what’s been said in the literature in relation to your point, if you have already identified one. If you haven’t identified a point yet, what point could you make? As you read through all the sources for BLUE, what are the central issues arising in this area?
  • How can you begin to apply some critical analysis to what’s in front of you? Read our additional information on critical prompts to help you. Make notes of the answers as you read.
  • Do you need to lose anything at this stage because it’s an old study, not a reliable source, or it repeats something elsewhere? If so, remove the post-it note and cross it off your plan.
  • Repeat these tasks for each of the colours on your map.

ADDITIONAL READING

Thinking critically about what you read involves asking questions about it. You might not ask the same questions of what you read each time, depending on what kind of source it is, but it’s useful to give yourself critical prompts. Academic researchers who have been reading material from their field for many years do this automatically. To help you adopt the same questioning mind you can devise your own list of critical prompts. Get a notebook just for making this list. Use the suggestions given here to get you going, but ADD to it. The more practice you have at critical reading, the more your list will develop until you no longer need it.

Critical Prompts:

  • What’s the author’s position?
  • What’s the main argument or hypothesis?
  • Does the conclusion have adequate supporting reasons?
  • Does the supporting evidence seem weak or strong?
  • Can you identify flaws in the argument?
  • What works and what doesn’t? Can you apply constructive criticism? Or suggest recommendations that may have yielded different results?
  • How does it compare to other sources on the same subject?
  • Who agrees? Who disagrees? Why?
  • What’s the significance of what’s being said?
  • What are the limitations of the study/what’s being said?
  • How and when do these limitations occur?
  • How does it advance our understanding of the subject?
  • What methods were used to test the hypotheses? Were they the best choice?
  • Were the sample sizes, types, controls for variables reasonable?
  • Are conclusions drawn on the basis of the research flawed or accurate?
  • How old is the research? Has it been replaced with more up to date research?
  • If the research is old, does it still have value?
TIPS
  • 1. This step also helps you to filter out the irrelevant material, the evidence that sees you going off on a tangent and that might – although interesting – belong in another essay.
  • 2. Each point you have identified on your Structure Map is potentially a paragraph in your essay. The next steps will help you flesh these out and put them in some kind of order. Paragraphs are merely the building blocks of essays. You stack them up in the most persuasive and logical order and you have an essay. Look at what you have already – lots of blocks to build with. You’re getting there!
