ABOUT THIS STEP
xxxxxx by Dr Anna Barker. Length: xxxx minutes

This essay-writing tool has two stages of reading: a wide read comes first and gathers information from the field. It’s skim reading rather than close reading. It pulls together only what is relevant. Then, second, a close read. This second read is slower and concentrates more on critically thinking about the sources you have gathered and the correlations between these different ‘voices’ in the literature.

TIPS

You can use colours to log relevant information that’s in your notebook too. Made some notes in a lecture that’s relevant in some way to your essay? Highlight the text in the colour that corresponds to that theme in your essay.

TASKS
  • Read from varying sources, read for anything you think will help support the points you have already identified in your brainstorm. As you read look for new points you can make. The deep thinking comes later, this first read is about surveying the field and pulling in what’s relevant to underpin your points. You’ll absorb more than you think, but most importantly you’ll give yourself access to a broad range of the literature available.
  • If you find something useful stick a post-it note on the page in the colour that corresponds to the theme on your map. If it’s online, print it out.
  • Write the page number and the title of the journal/book on your Structure Map underneath your point. Then put the book back on your shelf or if it is a loose sheet, put it in the magazine folder.

NB: Every time you come across something which is relevant to a theme of the essay, enables you to make a point, or underpins a point you have already identified in your brainstorm then you should code it in the appropriate colour and put both the point and the reference on your Structure Map.
